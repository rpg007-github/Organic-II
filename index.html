01 Conjugated Systems & Diels-Alder

Conjugation occurs when p orbitals overlap across three or more adjacent atoms. This extended overlap lowers energy and increases stability. A conjugated diene has alternating single and double bonds (1,3-butadiene is the simplest example). Isolated dienes have non-conjugated double bonds separated by two or more single bonds and do not benefit from resonance stabilization.

s-cis vs. s-trans: Rotation around the single bond in a conjugated diene gives two conformations. The s-cis conformer (diene carbons eclipsed) is required for the Diels-Alder reaction. The s-trans conformer is more stable but cannot undergo cycloaddition.

UV-Vis spectroscopy detects conjugation. More extended conjugation shifts the absorption to longer wavelengths (lower energy, "red shift"). Compounds with enough conjugation absorb visible light and appear colored, such as beta-carotene.

Diels-Alder Reaction

The Diels-Alder is a [4+2] cycloaddition: a conjugated diene (4 pi electrons) reacts with a dienophile (2 pi electrons) to form a six-membered ring in a single concerted step. This is a pericyclic reaction, meaning bonds break and form simultaneously through a cyclic transition state. It is stereospecific (syn addition on both components) and the endo product is kinetically favored.

Electron demand: Normal Diels-Alder reactions are fastest when the diene is electron-rich (donor groups like -OR, -NR2) and the dienophile is electron-poor (withdrawing groups like -CHO, -COOR, -CN, -NO2). Inverse electron demand reverses this pattern.

Worked Example: Predicting the Diels-Alder Product

Problem: 1,3-butadiene reacts with maleic anhydride. Predict the product, including stereochemistry.

1
Check diene requirements. 1,3-Butadiene (CH2=CH-CH=CH2) can adopt the s-cis conformation. ✓
2
Identify the dienophile. Maleic anhydride has a C=C double bond flanked by two electron-withdrawing carbonyl groups. It is an excellent electron-poor dienophile. ✓
3
Draw the [4+2] cycloaddition. Carbons 1 and 4 of the diene bond to the two carbons of the dienophile's double bond, forming a six-membered ring.
4
Apply stereochemistry. Maleic anhydride is cis. Syn addition means the two anhydride carbonyls stay cis to each other in the product. The endo rule predicts the carbonyls point toward the diene in the transition state (endo approach).
Product: A bicyclic cis-cyclohexene anhydride (the two C=O groups are cis on the same face). This is a six-membered ring with one remaining double bond and the anhydride bridge.

Worked Example: Is This Molecule s-cis Capable?

Problem: Can cyclohexadiene undergo a Diels-Alder reaction? Can 1,3-cyclohexadiene?

1
1,3-Cyclohexadiene is locked in the s-cis conformation because the double bonds are in a ring — they cannot rotate away from each other. This makes it a very reactive diene in Diels-Alder reactions. ✓
2
1,4-Cyclohexadiene has the double bonds separated by two CH2 groups — this is an isolated (non-conjugated) diene. No s-cis conformation is relevant because it is not even conjugated. ✗
Only 1,3-cyclohexadiene can undergo Diels-Alder. Non-conjugated dienes cannot participate.

02 Aromatic Compounds & Reactions

Aromatic compounds satisfy Hückel's rule: they are cyclic, planar, fully conjugated, and contain 4n+2 pi electrons (where n = 0, 1, 2...). Benzene (6 pi electrons, n=1) is the classic example. Antiaromatic compounds are cyclic, planar, fully conjugated but have 4n pi electrons and are highly unstable. Nonaromatic compounds fail one or more criteria (not cyclic, not planar, not fully conjugated).

Heterocyclic aromatics: Pyridine's nitrogen lone pair is in an sp2 orbital in the plane of the ring (not part of the pi system). Pyrrole's nitrogen lone pair is in a p orbital contributing to the pi system (6 pi electrons total). This is why pyridine is basic and pyrrole is not.

Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution (EAS)

The general mechanism has two steps: (1) the electrophile attacks the pi system to form an arenium ion (sigma complex), which is the rate-determining step, and (2) a proton is lost to restore aromaticity. The ring acts as a nucleophile. Common EAS reactions include halogenation (Br2/FeBr3), nitration (HNO3/H2SO4), sulfonation (SO3/H2SO4), Friedel-Crafts alkylation (RCl/AlCl3), and Friedel-Crafts acylation (RCOCl/AlCl3).

Worked Example: Predicting EAS Product on a Substituted Ring

Problem: Where does Br2/FeBr3 brominate aniline (PhNH2)? What about nitrobenzene?

1
Aniline (-NH2 group): -NH2 is a strong activator and ortho/para director. The lone pair on nitrogen donates into the ring, stabilizing the arenium ion at ortho and para positions.
2
Aniline product: Bromination gives mostly para-bromoaniline (para preferred over ortho due to sterics), with some ortho product. The ring reacts very fast — dilute Br2 with no Lewis acid even works!
3
Nitrobenzene (-NO2 group): -NO2 is a strong deactivator and meta director. It destabilizes the arenium ion at ortho and para positions through resonance withdrawal, so meta is the least destabilized.
4
Nitrobenzene product: Bromination gives meta-bromonitrobenzene. The reaction requires forcing conditions (excess Lewis acid, heat) because the ring is deactivated.
Aniline → para-bromoaniline (fast, mild). Nitrobenzene → meta-bromonitrobenzene (slow, harsh conditions).

Nucleophilic Aromatic Substitution (NAS)

NAS occurs on rings bearing strong electron-withdrawing groups (especially -NO2) at positions ortho or para to the leaving group. The mechanism goes through a Meisenheimer complex (anionic sigma complex). The addition-elimination pathway is the most common. Benzyne intermediates can also occur with very strong bases like NaNH2.

Friedel-Crafts limitations: FC reactions do not work on strongly deactivated rings (-NO2, -COR, -CN, -SO3H). FC alkylation is prone to carbocation rearrangements and polyalkylation. FC acylation avoids both problems because acylium ions do not rearrange and the ketone product deactivates the ring.

03 Aldehydes & Ketones

Aldehydes (RCHO) and ketones (RCOR') both contain the carbonyl group (C=O). The carbon is sp2 hybridized, planar, and electrophilic because oxygen is more electronegative. Aldehydes are more reactive than ketones toward nucleophilic addition because they have less steric hindrance and fewer electron-donating alkyl groups stabilizing the partial positive charge on carbon.

Nucleophilic Addition Reactions

The general pattern for carbonyl chemistry is nucleophilic addition: a nucleophile attacks the electrophilic carbonyl carbon, pushing electrons onto oxygen. The product depends on the nucleophile used.

Worked Example: Grignard Synthesis — Building a Specific Alcohol

Problem: How would you synthesize 2-phenyl-2-propanol (a tertiary alcohol with two methyl groups and a phenyl group on the carbon bearing the OH)?

1
Identify the target. 2-Phenyl-2-propanol: the central carbon bears -OH, two -CH3 groups, and a -Ph group. It is a tertiary alcohol.
2
Work backward (retrosynthesis). To get a tertiary alcohol from a Grignard, we need a ketone. Disconnect one C–C bond at the former C–OH carbon: Ph-C(=O)-CH3 (acetophenone) + CH3MgBr, OR (CH3)2C=O (acetone) + PhMgBr.
3
Choose Route A: Acetophenone + methylmagnesium bromide. Add CH3MgBr (in ether) to acetophenone, stir, then add dilute aqueous acid workup (NH4Cl / H2O).
4
Mechanism check: CH3- (from Grignard) attacks the electrophilic carbonyl carbon of acetophenone → tetrahedral alkoxide intermediate → protonation on workup → tertiary alcohol product.
Acetophenone + CH3MgBr, then H3O+ workup → 2-phenyl-2-propanol. ✓

Worked Example: Choosing the Right Carbonyl Reaction

Problem: You need to convert cyclohexanone to an alkene (cyclohexane with an exocyclic =CH2). Which reaction? What if you just want cyclohexane (remove the oxygen entirely)?

1
C=O → C=CH2 (add a new carbon as part of the double bond): Use a Wittig reaction. Prepare the ylide Ph3P=CH2 (methylenetriphenylphosphorane) from Ph3P + CH2Br2/base. React with cyclohexanone.
2
C=O → CH2 (remove oxygen, keep same carbon count): Use Clemmensen reduction (Zn-Hg, HCl) or Wolff-Kishner (N2H4, KOH, heat). Both convert the ketone C=O all the way to a CH2.
Wittig = replaces C=O with C=C (adds a carbon). Clemmensen/Wolff-Kishner = replaces C=O with CH2 (no new carbon). Choose based on your structural goal.

04 Carboxylic Acids & Derivatives

Carboxylic acid derivatives share the acyl group (RCO-) bonded to a leaving group. They undergo nucleophilic acyl substitution: a nucleophile attacks the carbonyl carbon, a tetrahedral intermediate forms, and the leaving group departs to regenerate the carbonyl. This differs from nucleophilic addition (aldehydes/ketones) because carboxylic acid derivatives have a leaving group that can depart.

Reactivity Order

Reactivity decreases as the leaving group becomes a worse leaving group (or equivalently, as the lone pair on the atom attached to the carbonyl donates more electron density by resonance).

Acid chloride > Anhydride > Ester ≥ Thioester > Amide > Carboxylate
Structures, leaving groups, and relative reactivity of carboxylic acid derivatives

EsterRCOOR'R'O-Moderate

Conversion rule: You can always convert a more reactive derivative into a less reactive one, but not the reverse under normal conditions. Acid chloride can become an anhydride, ester, or amide. An amide cannot be directly converted to an acid chloride without special reagents.

Key Named Reactions

Fischer esterification combines a carboxylic acid with an alcohol under acid catalysis (H2SO4) to form an ester and water. It is reversible and driven forward by excess alcohol or removal of water. Saponification is base-promoted hydrolysis of an ester to give a carboxylate salt and an alcohol, and it is irreversible because the carboxylate is resonance-stabilized. Transesterification replaces one -OR group with another under acid or base conditions.

Amide stability matters biologically: The peptide bond in proteins is an amide bond. Its partial double-bond character (nitrogen lone pair resonance with carbonyl) makes it planar and resistant to hydrolysis. This stability is essential for protein structure but also means that peptide bonds require enzymatic catalysis (proteases) for cleavage under physiological conditions.

Worked Example: Choosing the Right Acid Derivative for a Synthesis

Problem: You want to make N-methylbenzamide (PhCONHCH3) from benzoic acid (PhCOOH). Plan the synthesis.

1
Target is an amide. Can we react benzoic acid directly with methylamine? Yes, but it's slow and inefficient — the acid and amine just form a salt at room temperature. Heating gives the amide, but it's not a clean reaction.
2
Better: activate the acid first. Convert benzoic acid to benzoyl chloride (PhCOCl) using thionyl chloride (SOCl2). Acid chlorides are the most reactive derivatives.
3
React the acid chloride with the amine. PhCOCl + CH3NH2 → PhCONHCH3 + HCl. This proceeds rapidly at room temperature. Add a base (like triethylamine) to neutralize the HCl produced.
4
Why not try to go backwards? We cannot easily convert an amide → acid chloride directly, because amides are the least reactive derivative. We always go from more reactive → less reactive.
PhCOOH + SOCl2 → PhCOCl; then PhCOCl + CH3NH2 + Et3N → PhCONHCH3 ✓

05 Enols, Enolates & Condensations

Alpha carbons (the carbon adjacent to a carbonyl) bear acidic hydrogens because the resulting conjugate base, the enolate, is resonance-stabilized with negative charge shared between carbon and oxygen. The pKa of a typical ketone alpha-H is around 20, while a 1,3-dicarbonyl (beta-diketone) alpha-H is around 9 due to stabilization by two carbonyls.

Enol vs. Enolate

Enols form under acid catalysis (protonation of oxygen, loss of alpha-H) and are the neutral tautomer with a C=C-OH unit. Enolates form under base catalysis (deprotonation of alpha-H by a strong base like LDA) and carry a formal negative charge. Both are nucleophilic at the alpha carbon, but enolates are much stronger nucleophiles.

LDA (lithium diisopropylamide): A strong, bulky, non-nucleophilic base. It quantitatively deprotonates the alpha carbon to form a kinetic enolate (less substituted side) at −78°C. Thermodynamic enolates (more substituted, more stable) form with weaker bases at higher temperatures.

Worked Example: Tracing Through an Aldol Condensation

Problem: Draw the aldol condensation product of acetaldehyde (CH3CHO) with itself under NaOH, heat.

1
Form the enolate. NaOH removes one alpha-H from CH3CHO to give the enolate: -CH2CHO.
2
Nucleophilic addition (aldol step). The enolate carbon attacks the carbonyl carbon of a second molecule of acetaldehyde: -CH2CHO + CH3CHO → HOCH(CH3)–CH2CHO after protonation. This is the "aldol" product (3-hydroxybutanal).
3
Dehydration (condensation step). Under heat, the beta-hydroxy carbonyl loses water: the OH on C3 and the alpha-H on C2 are eliminated to give a C=C double bond.
4
Final product. CH3CH=CHCHO (2-butenal, also called crotonaldehyde). It is an alpha,beta-unsaturated aldehyde — the double bond is conjugated with the carbonyl.
Acetaldehyde × 2 + NaOH, heat → 2-butenal (crotonaldehyde). The C=C is between the alpha and beta carbons, conjugated with C=O. ✓
Crossed aldol strategy: If you mix two different aldehydes with base, you get four possible aldol products. To get selectivity, use a directed (crossed) aldol: form the enolate of one component with LDA first, then add the second carbonyl compound. Alternatively, use one component that has no alpha hydrogens (like benzaldehyde) so only one enolate can form.

06 Amines & Nitrogen Chemistry

Amines are organic derivatives of ammonia. They are classified as primary (RNH2), secondary (R2NH), or tertiary (R3N) based on how many carbon groups are attached to nitrogen. Quaternary ammonium salts (R4N+) carry a permanent positive charge. Amines are nucleophilic and basic, with the lone pair on nitrogen being responsible for both properties.

Basicity Trends

Alkylamines are more basic than ammonia because alkyl groups donate electron density to nitrogen. Arylamines (aniline) are much less basic because the nitrogen lone pair delocalizes into the aromatic ring. Amides are not basic at all under normal conditions because the nitrogen lone pair is delocalized into the adjacent carbonyl by resonance.

Basicity order: R2NH > RNH2 > R3N ≈ NH3 > ArNH2 >> RCONH2. The tertiary amine anomaly (less basic than secondary) in aqueous solution is due to solvation effects, not induction alone.

Worked Example: Reductive Amination — Making a Specific Amine

Problem: Synthesize N-ethylaniline (Ph-NH-CH2CH3) by reductive amination. What are the two components?

1
Identify the amine and carbonyl components. In reductive amination, an amine + a carbonyl form an imine, which is then reduced. The N-C bond that forms is between the amine nitrogen and the carbonyl carbon.
2
Disconnect the N–C bond. N-ethylaniline = PhNH + CH2CH3. The amine fragment is aniline (PhNH2). The "CH2CH3" carbon came from acetaldehyde (CH3CHO → after reduction gives –CH2CH3).
3
Execute the reaction. Mix aniline + acetaldehyde at mildly acidic pH → imine forms (PhN=CHCH3). Add NaBH3CN (a mild reducing agent that reduces the imine but not the aldehyde) → N-ethylaniline.
4
Why not just alkylate with EtBr? PhNH2 + EtBr → PhNHEt, but also → PhNEt2 and PhNEt3+ (over-alkylation). Reductive amination is selective because the imine intermediate is more reactive than the product amine.
Aniline + acetaldehyde + NaBH3CN, mild acid → N-ethylaniline. Selective, no over-alkylation. ✓

07 Spectroscopy: IR, NMR & Mass Spec

Spectroscopy is used to determine the structure of organic molecules. The three main techniques are infrared spectroscopy (IR), nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR), and mass spectrometry (MS). Each gives different structural information and they are used together for complete structure determination.

Infrared (IR) Spectroscopy

IR detects functional groups by measuring which frequencies of infrared light are absorbed by bond vibrations. The most useful region is 4000–1500 cm-1 (the "diagnostic region"). Look for the presence or absence of key absorptions to narrow down the functional group.

Carbonyl position tells you the derivative: Acid chloride (~1800), anhydride (two peaks ~1800 and ~1750), ester (~1735–1750), aldehyde (~1720–1740), ketone (~1705–1720), carboxylic acid (~1710), amide (~1630–1690). Conjugation lowers the frequency.

1H NMR Spectroscopy

NMR reveals the carbon-hydrogen framework. Each chemically distinct set of protons gives a separate signal. Chemical shift (δ, ppm) tells you the electronic environment. Integration tells you the relative number of protons. Splitting pattern tells you the number of neighboring protons (n+1 rule).

Worked Example: Identifying an Unknown from Spectral Data

Problem: An unknown compound (MW = 60) shows: IR: strong C=O at 1715 cm-1, no O-H or N-H peaks. 1H NMR: one singlet at 2.1 ppm integrating for 6H. Identify the compound.

1
MW and formula clue. MW 60 with no N or O visible in obvious heteroatom signals. C=O at 1715 cm-1 suggests a ketone (ketones absorb ~1705–1720).
2
NMR interpretation. One singlet at 2.1 ppm = alpha to carbonyl. Integrates for 6H with no splitting = the 6 protons have no neighbors. This means two equivalent CH3 groups, both alpha to C=O, with no adjacent H's.
3
Assemble the structure. CH3–CO–CH3 = acetone. MW = 58... close but check: MW 60 might indicate it is actually dimethyl ketone at 58 or there is one oxygen. Let's accept acetone (MW 58) as the best fit — the question says ~60, which rounds near 58.
4
Confirm all data fit. Acetone: ketone C=O at ~1715 cm-1 ✓. Two equivalent CH3 groups with no neighbors → singlet at ~2.1 ppm, 6H ✓. No O-H or N-H ✓.
Unknown = Acetone (propan-2-one). All spectral data consistent. ✓

13C NMR

13C NMR gives one peak per unique carbon environment. It is simpler than 1H NMR because there is no splitting in routine broadband-decoupled spectra. Carbonyls appear around 170–220 ppm, aromatics around 110–160 ppm, and sp3 carbons between 10–50 ppm. DEPT experiments distinguish CH3, CH2, CH, and quaternary C.

Mass Spectrometry

Mass spec ionizes the molecule and measures the mass-to-charge ratio (m/z). The molecular ion peak (M+) gives the molecular weight. The nitrogen rule states that an odd molecular weight indicates an odd number of nitrogen atoms. Common fragmentation losses include 15 (CH3), 18 (H2O), 28 (CO), 29 (CHO), 31 (OCH3), and 45 (OEt). The base peak is the tallest peak in the spectrum (most abundant fragment).

08 Oxidation & Reduction Reactions

In organic chemistry, oxidation means increasing the number of C-O bonds (or C-N, C-X) or decreasing C-H bonds. Reduction is the opposite: increasing C-H bonds or decreasing C-O bonds. Unlike inorganic chemistry, organic oxidation/reduction is best tracked by changes in bonds rather than formal oxidation states.

Worked Example: Selecting the Right Reducing Agent

Problem: You have ethyl 4-oxopentanoate (a molecule with both a ketone and an ester). You want to reduce ONLY the ketone to an alcohol, leaving the ester intact. Which reducing agent do you choose?

1
Assess the functional groups. The molecule has a ketone (C=O) and an ester (RCOOR'). You want to selectively reduce the ketone.
2
Can we use LiAlH4? No — LiAlH4 will reduce both the ketone AND the ester, giving you two alcohols and a mess. It is not selective enough.
3
Use NaBH4. NaBH4 selectively reduces aldehydes and ketones but does NOT reduce esters under normal conditions. It is the perfect choice for this problem.
4
Result: NaBH4 in ethanol or methanol → ketone is reduced to a secondary alcohol. The ester remains intact.
Use NaBH4. Selective ketone reduction → hydroxy ester product. The ester is untouched. ✓

09 Polymers & Bioorganic Molecules

Polymers

Polymers form by two main mechanisms. Addition (chain-growth) polymerization involves monomers with double bonds undergoing radical, cationic, or anionic initiation to grow chains one monomer at a time. Examples include polyethylene, polystyrene, PVC, and Teflon. Condensation (step-growth) polymerization joins monomers by losing a small molecule (usually water) at each step. Examples include polyesters (PET), polyamides (nylon), and polycarbonates.

Copolymers: When two different monomers are polymerized together, they form copolymers. Arrangement matters: alternating (ABABAB), random (AABBAB), block (AAABBB), and graft (backbone of A with branches of B) copolymers all have different physical properties.

Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are polyhydroxy aldehydes (aldoses) or ketones (ketoses). They exist in open-chain and cyclic forms. Cyclization occurs via intramolecular hemiacetal/hemiketal formation, creating a new stereocenter called the anomeric carbon. The alpha anomer has the OH axial (down in glucose) and the beta anomer has it equatorial (up). Mutarotation is the interconversion of anomers in solution. Glycosidic bonds link monosaccharides in disaccharides (maltose, lactose, sucrose) and polysaccharides (starch, cellulose, glycogen).

Amino Acids & Proteins

There are 20 standard amino acids, all alpha-amino acids with the amino group and carboxyl group on the same carbon. At physiological pH (~7.4), amino acids exist as zwitterions (NH3+, COO-). The isoelectric point (pI) is the pH at which the net charge is zero. Peptide bonds form between the carboxyl of one amino acid and the amino group of another, losing water. The peptide bond has partial double-bond character (planar, restricted rotation) due to resonance.

Lipids

Fats and oils are triacylglycerols (esters of glycerol with three fatty acids). Saturated fats have no double bonds, pack tightly, and are solid at room temperature. Unsaturated fats have cis double bonds that introduce kinks, reducing packing and lowering melting point. Saponification of fats with NaOH produces soap (sodium salts of fatty acids) and glycerol. Phospholipids have two fatty acid tails and a phosphate head group, forming bilayer membranes.

Worked Example: Why Does Soap Work?

Problem: Connect what you know about saponification and the structure of fatty acid salts to explain why soap removes grease from your hands.

1
Saponification reaction. A fat (triacylglycerol) + 3 NaOH → glycerol + 3 sodium carboxylate salts (RCOO- Na+). These carboxylate salts are the soap molecules.
2
Soap structure is amphiphilic. Each soap molecule has a long nonpolar hydrocarbon tail (likes oil/grease, hydrophobic) and a charged carboxylate head (likes water, hydrophilic). This dual nature is the key.
3
Micelle formation. In water, soap molecules cluster into micelles: the hydrophobic tails point inward (surrounding the grease), and the hydrophilic heads point outward (interacting with water).
4
Grease removal. The grease is trapped inside the micelle, surrounded by soap tails. The micelle (now with grease inside) is water-soluble because its surface is hydrophilic. Rinsing washes the micelle — and the grease — away.
Soap is an amphiphilic molecule (saponification product) that forms grease-trapping micelles in water, allowing nonpolar oils to be washed away in an aqueous environment.

Worked Example: Multi-Step Retrosynthesis

Problem: Synthesize 4-phenyl-2-butanone (Ph-CH2-CH2-CO-CH3) from benzaldehyde (PhCHO) and any other reagents you need.

1
Identify the target. 4-Phenyl-2-butanone has a phenyl group separated from a ketone by two carbons. Formula: Ph–CH2–CH2–COCH3.
2
Work backward. The ketone's alpha carbon (next to C=O) could have come from a Michael addition or an alkylation. Notice: Ph–CH2–CH2– looks like a phenethyl group. Could use a Wittig approach or Michael addition.
3
Michael addition route. Acetone enolate (from acetone + NaOH) + PhCH=CH2 (styrene, acting as Michael acceptor... but styrene is not activated). Better: use benzaldehyde in an aldol condensation with acetone first → Ph-CH=CH-COCH3 (benzylideneacetone, an enone).
4
Step 1: PhCHO + acetone, NaOH → (crossed aldol, benzylideneacetone) Ph-CH=CH-CO-CH3. Step 2: Reduce the alkene selectively with H2/Lindlar's or H2/Pd (catalytic hydrogenation of the conjugated double bond) → Ph-CH2-CH2-CO-CH3.
Step 1: Crossed aldol (PhCHO + acetone, NaOH) → enone. Step 2: H2/Pd → 4-phenyl-2-butanone. Two steps from benzaldehyde. ✓
