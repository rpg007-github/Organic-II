01 Conjugated Systems & Diels-Alder

Conjugation occurs when p orbitals overlap across three or more adjacent atoms. This extended overlap lowers energy and increases stability. A conjugated diene has alternating single and double bonds (1,3-butadiene is the simplest example). Isolated dienes have non-conjugated double bonds separated by two or more single bonds and do not benefit from resonance stabilization.

s-cis vs. s-trans: Rotation around the single bond in a conjugated diene gives two conformations. The s-cis conformer (diene carbons eclipsed) is required for the Diels-Alder reaction. The s-trans conformer is more stable but cannot undergo cycloaddition.

UV-Vis spectroscopy detects conjugation. More extended conjugation shifts the absorption to longer wavelengths (lower energy, "red shift"). Compounds with enough conjugation absorb visible light and appear colored, such as beta-carotene.

Diels-Alder Reaction

The Diels-Alder is a [4+2] cycloaddition: a conjugated diene (4 pi electrons) reacts with a dienophile (2 pi electrons) to form a six-membered ring in a single concerted step. This is a pericyclic reaction, meaning bonds break and form simultaneously through a cyclic transition state. It is stereospecific (syn addition on both components) and the endo product is kinetically favored.

Electron demand: Normal Diels-Alder reactions are fastest when the diene is electron-rich (donor groups like -OR, -NR2) and the dienophile is electron-poor (withdrawing groups like -CHO, -COOR, -CN, -NO2). Inverse electron demand reverses this pattern.

02 Aromatic Compounds & Reactions

Aromatic compounds satisfy Huckel's rule: they are cyclic, planar, fully conjugated, and contain 4n+2 pi electrons (where n = 0, 1, 2...). Benzene (6 pi electrons, n=1) is the classic example. Antiaromatic compounds are cyclic, planar, fully conjugated but have 4n pi electrons and are highly unstable. Nonaromatic compounds fail one or more criteria (not cyclic, not planar, not fully conjugated).

Heterocyclic aromatics: Pyridine's nitrogen lone pair is in an sp2 orbital in the plane of the ring (not part of the pi system). Pyrrole's nitrogen lone pair is in a p orbital contributing to the pi system (6 pi electrons total). This is why pyridine is basic and pyrrole is not.

Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution (EAS)

The general mechanism has two steps: (1) the electrophile attacks the pi system to form an arenium ion (sigma complex), which is the rate-determining step, and (2) a proton is lost to restore aromaticity. The ring acts as a nucleophile. Common EAS reactions include halogenation (Br2/FeBr3), nitration (HNO3/H2SO4), sulfonation (SO3/H2SO4), Friedel-Crafts alkylation (RCl/AlCl3), and Friedel-Crafts acylation (RCOCl/AlCl3).

Halogens are the exception: They are ortho/para directors but deactivators. Their lone pairs donate by resonance (ortho/para directing) but their high electronegativity withdraws by induction (deactivating). The resonance effect controls regiochemistry while induction controls rate.

Nucleophilic Aromatic Substitution (NAS)

NAS occurs on rings bearing strong electron-withdrawing groups (especially -NO2) at positions ortho or para to the leaving group. The mechanism goes through a Meisenheimer complex (anionic sigma complex). The addition-elimination pathway is the most common. Benzyne intermediates can also occur with very strong bases like NaNH2.

Friedel-Crafts limitations: FC reactions do not work on strongly deactivated rings (-NO2, -COR, -CN, -SO3H). FC alkylation is prone to carbocation rearrangements and polyalkylation. FC acylation avoids both problems because acylium ions do not rearrange and the ketone product deactivates the ring.

03 Aldehydes & Ketones

Aldehydes (RCHO) and ketones (RCOR') both contain the carbonyl group (C=O). The carbon is sp2 hybridized, planar, and electrophilic because oxygen is more electronegative. Aldehydes are more reactive than ketones toward nucleophilic addition because they have less steric hindrance and fewer electron-donating alkyl groups stabilizing the partial positive charge on carbon.

Nucleophilic Addition Reactions

The general pattern for carbonyl chemistry is nucleophilic addition: a nucleophile attacks the electrophilic carbonyl carbon, pushing electrons onto oxygen. The product depends on the nucleophile used.

Acetal protecting groups: Acetals are stable under basic and neutral conditions but cleave in aqueous acid. This makes them ideal for protecting carbonyls when you need to do a reaction elsewhere in the molecule under basic conditions (like a Grignard). Remove the protecting group afterward with dilute acid.
Grignard rule of thumb: Count the carbons. Formaldehyde + RMgBr gives a primary alcohol. Any other aldehyde + RMgBr gives a secondary alcohol. A ketone + RMgBr gives a tertiary alcohol. The Grignard adds the R group to the carbonyl carbon.

04 Carboxylic Acids & Derivatives

Carboxylic acid derivatives share the acyl group (RCO-) bonded to a leaving group. They undergo nucleophilic acyl substitution: a nucleophile attacks the carbonyl carbon, a tetrahedral intermediate forms, and the leaving group departs to regenerate the carbonyl. This differs from nucleophilic addition (aldehydes/ketones) because carboxylic acid derivatives have a leaving group that can depart.

Reactivity Order

Reactivity decreases as the leaving group becomes a worse leaving group (or equivalently, as the lone pair on the atom attached to the carbonyl donates more electron density by resonance).

Acid chloride > Anhydride > Ester ≥ Thioester > Amide > Carboxylate
DerivativeStructureLeaving GroupRelative Reactivity
Acid chlorideRCOClCl-Most reactive

EsterRCOOR'R'O-Moderate
ThioesterRCOSR'R'S-Moderate

Conversion rule: You can always convert a more reactive derivative into a less reactive one, but not the reverse under normal conditions. Acid chloride can become an anhydride, ester, or amide. An amide cannot be directly converted to an acid chloride without special reagents.

Key Named Reactions

Fischer esterification combines a carboxylic acid with an alcohol under acid catalysis (H2SO4) to form an ester and water. It is reversible and driven forward by excess alcohol or removal of water. Saponification is base-promoted hydrolysis of an ester to give a carboxylate salt and an alcohol, and it is irreversible because the carboxylate is resonance-stabilized. Transesterification replaces one -OR group with another under acid or base conditions.

Amide stability matters biologically: The peptide bond in proteins is an amide bond. Its partial double-bond character (nitrogen lone pair resonance with carbonyl) makes it planar and resistant to hydrolysis. This stability is essential for protein structure but also means that peptide bonds require enzymatic catalysis (proteases) for cleavage under physiological conditions.

05 Enols, Enolates & Condensations

Alpha carbons (the carbon adjacent to a carbonyl) bear acidic hydrogens because the resulting conjugate base, the enolate, is resonance-stabilized with negative charge shared between carbon and oxygen. The pKa of a typical ketone alpha-H is around 20, while a 1,3-dicarbonyl (beta-diketone) alpha-H is around 9 due to stabilization by two carbonyls.

Enol vs. Enolate

Enols form under acid catalysis (protonation of oxygen, loss of alpha-H) and are the neutral tautomer with a C=C-OH unit. Enolates form under base catalysis (deprotonation of alpha-H by a strong base like LDA) and carry a formal negative charge. Both are nucleophilic at the alpha carbon, but enolates are much stronger nucleophiles.

LDA (lithium diisopropylamide): A strong, bulky, non-nucleophilic base. It quantitatively deprotonates the alpha carbon to form a kinetic enolate (less substituted side) at -78°C. Thermodynamic enolates (more substituted, more stable) form with weaker bases at higher temperatures.

Mnemonic for aldol: "AL-DOL" = ALdehyde + alDOL (aldehyde acts as both nucleophile via enolate and electrophile via carbonyl). Product always has a beta-hydroxy carbonyl (aldol) that can dehydrate to conjugated enone.
Crossed aldol strategy: If you mix two different aldehydes with base, you get four possible aldol products. To get selectivity, use a directed (crossed) aldol: form the enolate of one component with LDA first, then add the second carbonyl compound. Alternatively, use one component that has no alpha hydrogens (like benzaldehyde) so only one enolate can form.

06 Amines & Nitrogen Chemistry

Amines are organic derivatives of ammonia. They are classified as primary (RNH2), secondary (R2NH), or tertiary (R3N) based on how many carbon groups are attached to nitrogen. Quaternary ammonium salts (R4N+) carry a permanent positive charge. Amines are nucleophilic and basic, with the lone pair on nitrogen being responsible for both properties.

Basicity Trends

Alkylamines are more basic than ammonia because alkyl groups donate electron density to nitrogen. Arylamines (aniline) are much less basic because the nitrogen lone pair delocalizes into the aromatic ring. Amides are not basic at all under normal conditions because the nitrogen lone pair is delocalized into the adjacent carbonyl by resonance.

Basicity order: R2NH > RNH2 > R3N ≈ NH3 > ArNH2 >> RCONH2. The tertiary amine anomaly (less basic than secondary) in aqueous solution is due to solvation effects, not induction alone.

Diazonium versatility: Aryl diazonium salts (ArN2+) are incredibly useful synthetic intermediates. They can be converted to ArOH (H2O/heat), ArF (HBF4/heat, Schiemann), ArCl or ArBr (CuCl or CuBr, Sandmeyer), ArCN (CuCN, Sandmeyer), ArI (KI), ArH (H3PO2), or coupled with activated rings (azo coupling for dyes). This makes -NH2 an ultimate directing group: install it for ortho/para direction, then replace it with whatever you need via the diazonium.

07 Spectroscopy: IR, NMR & Mass Spec

Spectroscopy is used to determine the structure of organic molecules. The three main techniques in Organic II are infrared spectroscopy (IR), nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR), and mass spectrometry (MS). Each gives different structural information and they are used together for complete structure determination.

Infrared (IR) Spectroscopy

IR detects functional groups by measuring which frequencies of infrared light are absorbed by bond vibrations. The most useful region is 4000-1500 cm-1 (the "diagnostic region"). Look for the presence or absence of key absorptions to narrow down the functional group.

Absorption (cm-1)Bond / GroupAppearance
3200-3600O-H (alcohol, carboxylic acid)Broad; acid is very broad (2500-3300)
3300-3500N-H (amine, amide)1° amine: two peaks; 2°: one peak
3300C-H (sp, alkyne)Sharp, strong
2850-3000C-H (sp3, sp2)Always present
2100-2260C≡C or C≡NWeak (alkyne); medium (nitrile)
1700-1750C=O (carbonyl)Strong, sharp; exact position indicates type
1600-1680C=C (alkene, aromatic)Variable intensity
Carbonyl position tells you the derivative: Acid chloride (~1800), anhydride (two peaks ~1800 and ~1750), ester (~1735-1750), aldehyde (~1720-1740), ketone (~1705-1720), carboxylic acid (~1710), amide (~1630-1690). Conjugation lowers the frequency.

1H NMR Spectroscopy

NMR reveals the carbon-hydrogen framework. Each chemically distinct set of protons gives a separate signal. Chemical shift (delta, ppm) tells you the electronic environment. Integration tells you the relative number of protons. Splitting pattern tells you the number of neighboring protons (n+1 rule).

Splitting pattern reminder: n+1 rule: if a proton has n equivalent neighboring H's, it splits into n+1 peaks. A triplet means 2 neighbors. A quartet means 3 neighbors. Equivalent protons do not split each other.

13C NMR

13C NMR gives one peak per unique carbon environment. It is simpler than 1H NMR because there is no splitting in routine broadband-decoupled spectra. Carbonyls appear around 170-220 ppm, aromatics around 110-160 ppm, and sp3 carbons between 10-50 ppm. DEPT experiments distinguish CH3, CH2, CH, and quaternary C.

Mass Spectrometry

Mass spec ionizes the molecule and measures the mass-to-charge ratio (m/z). The molecular ion peak (M+) gives the molecular weight. The nitrogen rule states that an odd molecular weight indicates an odd number of nitrogen atoms. Common fragmentation losses include 15 (CH3), 18 (H2O), 28 (CO), 29 (CHO), 31 (OCH3), and 45 (OEt). The base peak is the tallest peak in the spectrum (most abundant fragment).

08 Oxidation & Reduction Reactions

In organic chemistry, oxidation means increasing the number of C-O bonds (or C-N, C-X) or decreasing C-H bonds. Reduction is the opposite: increasing C-H bonds or decreasing C-O bonds. Unlike inorganic chemistry, organic oxidation/reduction is best tracked by changes in bonds rather than formal oxidation states.

09 Polymers & Bioorganic Molecules

Polymers

Polymers form by two main mechanisms. Addition (chain-growth) polymerization involves monomers with double bonds undergoing radical, cationic, or anionic initiation to grow chains one monomer at a time. Examples include polyethylene, polystyrene, PVC, and Teflon. Condensation (step-growth) polymerization joins monomers by losing a small molecule (usually water) at each step. Examples include polyesters (PET), polyamides (nylon), and polycarbonates.

Copolymers: When two different monomers are polymerized together, they form copolymers. Arrangement matters: alternating (ABABAB), random (AABBAB), block (AAABBB), and graft (backbone of A with branches of B) copolymers all have different physical properties.

Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are polyhydroxy aldehydes (aldoses) or ketones (ketoses). They exist in open-chain and cyclic forms. Cyclization occurs via intramolecular hemiacetal/hemiketal formation, creating a new stereocenter called the anomeric carbon. The alpha anomer has the OH axial (down in glucose) and the beta anomer has it equatorial (up). Mutarotation is the interconversion of anomers in solution. Glycosidic bonds link monosaccharides in disaccharides (maltose, lactose, sucrose) and polysaccharides (starch, cellulose, glycogen).

Amino Acids & Proteins

There are 20 standard amino acids, all alpha-amino acids with the amino group and carboxyl group on the same carbon. At physiological pH (~7.4), amino acids exist as zwitterions (NH3+, COO-). The isoelectric point (pI) is the pH at which the net charge is zero. Peptide bonds form between the carboxyl of one amino acid and the amino group of another, losing water. The peptide bond has partial double-bond character (planar, restricted rotation) due to resonance.

Lipids

Fats and oils are triacylglycerols (esters of glycerol with three fatty acids). Saturated fats have no double bonds, pack tightly, and are solid at room temperature. Unsaturated fats have cis double bonds that introduce kinks, reducing packing and lowering melting point. Saponification of fats with NaOH produces soap (sodium salts of fatty acids) and glycerol. Phospholipids have two fatty acid tails and a phosphate head group, forming bilayer membranes.
